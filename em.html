The formulas included here are just those which are not in those pages, or are important for the course which may or may not be covered in the book (looking at you Plasma Physics).

1 Electrostatics

1.1 Potential in terms of Dipole Moment

The vector can be rewritten as: \[ V = \frac{\mathbf{\hat{p}}\cdot\mathbf{\hat{r}}}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r^2}\]

1.2 Dipole Moment

D.M. of a collection of point charges is given by: \[\mathbf{p} = \sum_{i=1}^n q_i \mathbf{r}'_i\] where \(r')\ is the distance from the origin.

2 Magnetostatics

2.1 Current

2.1.1 Surface Current (K)

\[ \mathbf{K} = \frac{d\mathbf{I}}{dl_\perp} \qquad \mathbf{K} = \sigma \mathbf{v} \]

2.1.2 Volume Current (J)

\[ \mathbf{J} = \frac{d\mathbf{I}}{da_\perp} \qquad \mathbf{J} = \rho\mathbf{v} \]

2.2 Magnetic Potential

2.2.1 Vector Potential

\[ \nabla \times \mathbf{A} = \mathbf{B} \]

2.2.2 Poisson Equation and Formulation

\begin{align*} \nabla^2\mathbf{A} &= -\mu_0 \mathbf{J}\\ \mathbf{A(r)} &= \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int \frac{\mathbf{J(r')}}{\mathit{r}}d\tau' \end{align*}

3 Electric Fields in Matter

3.1 Volume and Surface Bound Charges

\[ \mathbf{\rho_b} =-\nabla\cdot\mathbf{P} \qquad \mathbf{\sigma_b} = \mathbf{P}\cdot\mathbf{\hat{n}}\]

3.2 Electric Displacement

\begin{align*} \nabla \cdot \mathbf{D} &= \rho \\ \oint \mathbf{D}\cdot d\mathbf{a} &= Q_\text{fenc}\\ D &\equiv \epsilon_0 \mathbf{E} + \mathbf{P} \end{align*}

3.3 Polarization

For linear dielectrics: \[ \mathbf{P} = \epsilon_0\chi_e\mathbf{E}\] where \(\chi_e\) is the electric susceptibility.

Additionally, substituting this into the definition for electric displacement gives: \[ \mathbf{D} = \epsilon\mathbf{E} = \epsilon_0\epsilon_r \mathbf{E} = \epsilon_0(1+\chi_e)\mathbf{E}\]

4 Magnetic Fields in Matter

4.1 Volume and Surface Currents

\[ \mathbf{J_b} = \nabla\times\mathbf{M} \qquad \mathbf{K_b} = \mathbf{M} \times \mathbf{\hat{n}}\]

4.2 Ampere's Law in Matter

\begin{align*} \nabla\times\mathbf{H} &= \mathbf{J_f}\\ \oint \mathbf{H} \cdot d\mathbf{I} &= I_\text{fenc}\\ \mathbf{H} &\equiv \frac{1}{\mu_0}\mathbf{B} - \mathbf{M} \end{align*}

4.3 Examples

4.3.1 Field of Uniformly Magnetized Sphere

\[ \mathbf{B} = \frac23\mu_0\mathbf{M} \]

4.4 Magnetisation

For linear magnetic materials (paramagnetic and diamagnetic material) the magnetisation \(\mathbf{M}\) is proportional to the field \(\mathbf{B}\), but convention dictates it is written in terms of \(\mathbf{H}\) (which is determined solely by free current and as such traditionally been easy to identify i.e. read the current and you will know \(\mathbf{H}\)): \[ \mathbf{M} = \chi_m \mathbf{H}\] where \(\chi_m\) is known as the magnetic susceptibility.

Then: \[ \mathbf{B} = \mu_0(\mathbf{H}+\mathbf{M}) = \mu_0(1+\chi_m)\mathbf{H} = \mu_0\mu_r\mathbf{H} = \mu \mathbf{H}\]

5 Boundary Conditions

For not having free charges on the surface/interface (as is the case for reflection/refraction):

\begin{align*} \epsilon_1 E_1^\perp = \epsilon_2 E_2^\perp \quad & \quad \mathbf{E}^\|_1 = \mathbf{E}^\|_2 \\ B_1^\perp = B_2^\perp \quad & \quad \frac{1}{\mu_1}\mathbf{B}^\|_1 = \frac{1}{\mu_2}\mathbf{B}^\|_2 \end{align*}

6 Eletromagnetic Waves

6.1 Brewster's Angle

When \( r_\| = 0\) and \(r_\perp \neq 0\): \[ \alpha_B = tan^{-1}\left(\frac{n'}{n}\right)\] The transmitted ray angle would be: \[ \alpha ' = \frac\pi2 - \alpha_B\]

6.2 Refractive Index

\[ n = \frac{ck}{\omega}\]

6.3 Wavevector

If the wavevector \(k\) is real, there is no attenuation of EM waves. If it is imaginary, it causes the EM wave to pick up an attenuating function of distance, so attenuation occurs in conductors and plasma.

6.4 Velocities

Phase velocity: \[ v_p = \frac{\omega}{k} = \frac{c}{n}\] For complex refractive index consider only the real part.

Group velocity: \[ v_g = \frac{d\omega}{dk}\] Energy travels at group velocity so phase velocity can be faster than light.

6.5 Fresnel Equations

Usually deal with materials where \(\mu_r = 1\), so: \[ n = \sqrt{\frac{\epsilon\mu}{\epsilon_0\mu_0}} = \sqrt{\epsilon_r}\]

6.6 Relating E field and B field for plane waves

\[ \mathbf{\hat{B}} = \frac{1}{\omega} \mathbf{k}\times\mathbf{\hat{E}}\]

6.7 Pressure

Pressure from photon is derived from the momentum of a photon, and the flux is: \[ p = \frac{I}{c} = \frac{\langle N \rangle}{c}\] For reflected radiation: \[ p = 2\frac{I}{c} = \epsilon_0E_0^2\] Lose the factor of 2 if absorbed.

7 Plasma Physics

This is treated as an extension of EM waves into conducting media.

7.1 Definition

"A plasma is a state of matter containing an appreciable fraction of freely moving charged particles - they usually take the form or partly or wholly ionized gases. There are a sufficient number of these charged particles to cause the electromagnetic properties of the medium to be significantly different from those of solids, liquids or gases which are not ionized."

7.2 Useful equations

The dipole moment per unit volume is: \[ \mathbf{P} = -N_e e \mathbf{x}\] It can be shown that: \[ \mathbf{J} = \frac{\partial \mathbf{P}}{\partial t}\]
